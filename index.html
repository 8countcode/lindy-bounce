Optimal path (top ↔ bottom)

⏱ Tempo costs more than depth: the bottom impact grows with the square of tempo but only linearly with bounce size — so at fast tempos shrink the bounce rather than just speeding it up. And landing on one leg roughly doubles this limb's load, multiplying with the impact spike.

Example motions

⚖ Knee ↔ spine trade-off: a more forward, hip-dominant hinge lowers knee load but raises hip & lower-back load; a more upright, knee-dominant bounce does the reverse. Good technique keeps both moderate.

Technique cues

Protecting knees & back over years of dancing
  • Keep knees soft and springy — never lock them, never sink into a deep squat. Live in the small soft-knee range.
  • Track knees over the feet (the knee bends where the toes point). Caving inward or twisting a planted knee on swivels is the #1 knee risk — rotate from the hip and the ball of the foot.
  • Hinge at the hip with a long, neutral spine. The slight lean comes from the hips, not from rounding the lower back.
  • Absorb landings through the whole ankle–knee–hip chain, ball-of-foot first with the heel kissing the floor.
  • Let the foot do its job: roll onto the ball with an active, springy arch so the foot + toe flexors become an extra shock absorber that softens the hit to the knee and hip — but keep it controlled so the forefoot, plantar fascia and Achilles aren't overloaded.
  • Make the bounce smaller as tempo rises — at fast tempos it shrinks to a tiny internal compression.
  • Spring up off the floor (elastic rebound); don't sit down into the dip.
  • Keep weight forward on the balls of the feet with an engaged, braced core.
  • Keep feet under your center of mass — over-striding adds braking shear at the knee and a lumbar reach.
  • Condition calves, ankles and the posterior chain, and warm up before fast social dancing — the bounce is single-leg, forefoot-driven and high-frequency for hours.
